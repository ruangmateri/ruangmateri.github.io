39. why gravity is a free good ?


Not necessarily, although perhaps it is in most cases. In the event that you would need more gravity than Earth (or some other planet) provides to perform the utility-bearing task at hand, gravity would be considered a scarce good. One such example could be achieving higher explosive impact with a dropped explosive. A less violent possibility could include, say, life forms with dense bone/muscle structures from larger planets (i.e., higher gravity supply) seeking to adapt to Earth, or "Earthlings" adapting to life on planets such as Mars.

In the John Carter case, however, gravity may actually be an economic bad. The less gravity one is bound by, the more of a superhero he becomes and the more utility he derives (a bad is defined as a commodity that yields higher utility at lower consumption rates and vice versa). It also requires more energy to launch astronauts into space or for skaters to clear a gap. 

I believe you could mathematically establish a gravity-utility model by taking the first derivative of the work function of the desired activity with respect to a change in the force of gravity (mathematicians, feel free to correct me). The more mechanical work due to gravity one has to perform to accomplish a desired task, the less utility one gains from gravity. If that disutility outweighs any external benefits, gravity could in fact be considered a bad.

Thus, in the situations respectively described above, gravity could either be a scarce good or a free bad or even theoretically a neuter.
